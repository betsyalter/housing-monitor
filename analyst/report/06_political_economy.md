# Step 6 — Political Economy

> _Status: 2026-05-01. Default-driven first draft. ASSUMPTION-flagged
> on policy-leverage rankings._

The thesis transmits through policy in two directions. **Tailwinds**
are policies that mechanically reduce friction in the lock-in unwind
(Section 121, assumable mortgages, GSE reform). **Headwinds** are
policies that constrain the rate-path side (oil-driven inflation,
tariffs raising construction costs, MBS-spread distortions).

The political-economy lens treats policy as a **demand-condition input**
[per Investment Research Training Manual v5.0, L1126]: identify the
condition demand depends on, evaluate the durability of the
condition, and price the names as if the condition were not in place.

---

## Tailwind levers (ranked by leverage on thesis horizon)

### Lever 1 — Section 121 capital gains exclusion expansion
**Status:** Unchanged since 1997 ($250K single / $500K joint). Never
indexed.

**Mechanism.** Section 121 exempts up to $250K (single) / $500K
(joint) of primary-residence capital gains from federal taxation if
the seller has occupied the home 2 of the last 5 years. The
inflation-adjusted version of the 1997 number would be ~$510K /
$1.02M in 2026 dollars.

**Why this is the highest-leverage tailwind.** A coastal owner who
bought in 1997 at $300K and now has a $1.5M home is sitting on a
$1.2M gain. Above the $500K joint exclusion, the remaining $700K is
taxed at federal long-term capital gains (15-20%) plus state. That's
~$140K-$200K of incremental tax friction blocking the move-decision
— **larger than the rate-trade friction in many cases**. Expansion
to inflation-adjusted levels reduces or eliminates this friction.

**Bipartisan precedent.** Section 121 expansion has both Democratic
and Republican support — Democrats frame it as elderly-housing-
mobility; Republicans as tax relief. Bipartisan housing legislation
is rare, but Section 121 is on the rare side.

**Watch:** House Ways & Means committee, Senate Finance committee,
any bipartisan housing affordability bill. The Perplexity Computer
weekly task's legislative-scuttlebutt section monitors this directly.

**Expected direction:** + (positive for thesis).

**Expected timing:** Some chance in 2026 mid-terms or early 2027.
_ASSUMPTION:_ probability ~25% in the 18-24 month thesis horizon.

### Lever 2 — Assumable mortgage reform
**Status:** Currently limited to VA and FHA loans. Conventional
(GSE-conforming) loans are not assumable.

**Mechanism.** An assumable mortgage transfers seamlessly from
seller to buyer at the existing rate. The seller keeps proceeds; the
buyer inherits the sub-4% rate. **This bypasses the entire rate-
trade mechanism that creates the lock-in.**

**Why this is the second-highest-leverage tailwind.** Mechanical
direct attack on Factor 1. Even partial expansion (assumability for
specific loan types or vintages) would unlock material transaction
volume. Full expansion to all conventional loans would be the
single most thesis-positive policy event imaginable.

**Implementation path.** FHFA can authorize assumability for GSE-
conforming loans without congressional action. Treasury could
push FHFA in this direction as part of housing-affordability agenda.

**Constraints.** Assumability creates issues with mortgage servicing
rights, MBS pool composition, and lender economics. The mortgage
industry may oppose. Watch FHFA director and senior staff signals;
sell-side DC analysts (Cowen Washington Research, Capital Alpha)
typically have early reads on FHFA rule-making direction.

**Expected direction:** + (highest leverage on Factor 1 — mechanical).

**Expected timing:** _ASSUMPTION:_ probability ~15-25% in 18-24
months; very high impact if it happens.

### Lever 3 — GSE conservatorship exit / reform
**Status:** Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac in conservatorship since 2008.
Treasury holds senior preferred stock. Periodic discussion of exit
without resolution.

**Mechanism.** The 30-year fixed-rate mortgage is itself an artifact
of GSE structure (the GSEs absorb prepayment / interest-rate risk
that private capital wouldn't take at scale). GSE reform that
preserves the 30y fixed product is rate-neutral; GSE reform that
shifts toward private securitization could increase MBS spreads
(thesis-negative on rate transmission).

**Why this is medium-leverage.** Effects are bidirectional and
multi-year. A "clean" GSE exit that preserves the 30y product would
be neutral-to-positive (potentially compresses Treasury-MBS spread
by removing political-uncertainty premium). A botched exit could
widen spreads materially.

**Constraints.** Politically constrained on both sides. Treasury
has avoided the issue since 2018 specifically because the political
risk of a botched exit is high.

**Expected direction:** Mixed; **direction depends on form, not
just whether it happens.**

**Expected timing:** _ASSUMPTION:_ low probability in 18-24
months unless a major housing-affordability crisis forces action.

### Lever 4 — Down-payment assistance / first-time-buyer credit
**Status:** Various state-level programs; federal conversation
periodic. Most-recent federal proposal: $25K first-time buyer
credit (proposed 2024 election cycle).

**Mechanism.** Direct cash to qualifying first-time buyers attacks
Factor 4 (affordability ceiling) by reducing the upfront cash
requirement. JCHS 2025 documents only 12% of renters have $26,800
cash for closing on the median home — a $25K credit raises that
share materially.

**Why medium-leverage.** Operates on Factor 4, which has ~10% of
factor weight in the thesis (vs Factor 1's 40%). Substantial demand-
side relief but doesn't unlock the supply side.

**Constraints.** Cost: $25K × ~1M FTHBs/yr = $25B/yr. Politically
divisive. Likely tied to election cycle timing.

**Expected direction:** + (positive for absorption side of unlock).

**Expected timing:** _ASSUMPTION:_ election-cycle dependent; ~30-40%
probability in next 24 months given current administration's
housing-affordability framing.

### Lever 5 — VantageScore 4.0 / FICO 10T (already passed; ramping)
**Status:** **APPROVED 2026-04-22** [FHFA + HUD; see Perplexity weekly
output 2026-05-04]. Ramping through summer 2026 with historical FICO
10T data publication.

**Mechanism.** New credit-scoring methodologies incorporate rent-
payment history (VantageScore 4.0) and trended credit data (FICO
10T). Estimated to expand qualifiable-borrower pool by 4-7M
consumers over 3-5 years.

**Why this is already "in flight" — count it positively.** The
unlock thesis already has this tailwind locked in. Lenders adopt the
new score *if* GSE LLPA pricing makes it accretive — that pricing
publishes in summer 2026. **High probability of material adoption
through 2027.**

**Direction:** + (positive for absorption side of unlock — Factor 4
specifically).

### Lever 6 — 1031 exchange reform (deferred-gains rollover)
**Status:** Fully available; periodic discussion of caps.

**Mechanism.** Section 1031 allows deferral of capital gains tax on
real-estate-to-real-estate exchanges. Investor-held / second-home
properties use 1031 extensively. Restrictions (caps, narrowed
applicability) would force disposition of investor-held housing
into the market.

**Why low-medium leverage.** Affects ~$50-100B/yr of investor-real-
estate transaction volume; mostly investment property, not primary
residences. Direct impact on Factor 3 (2nd-home turnover) is
material but factor 3 is footnote-tier in the thesis.

**Direction:** + (positive for inventory release if narrowed).

**Expected timing:** _ASSUMPTION:_ low probability in 18-24 months.

---

### Lever 7 — Tax credit housing (LIHTC) expansion
**Status:** Permanent tax credit; periodic expansion discussions.

**Mechanism.** LIHTC expansion increases multifamily affordable
construction. **Direct headwind for apartment REITs (Tier-4 short
basket)** — increases multifamily supply at the lower-income tier.

**Why low-leverage relative to thesis.** LIHTC operates 4-7 years
out (development timeline). Thesis horizon is 18-36 months; LIHTC
expansion mostly affects post-thesis cycle.

**Direction:** + for thesis indirectly (rent disinflation supports
Factor 5); but timing is post-thesis.

## Headwind levers

### Headwind 1 — Tariffs on building materials
**Status:** Lumber and steel tariffs at elevated levels; potential
expansion to additional categories under current administration.

**Mechanism.** Building material tariffs raise input costs for
homebuilders. Tier-1 builders (DHI, NVR, LEN, PHM) typically pass
this through to ASP; Tier 2-3 builders absorb. **More importantly,
tariffs are inflationary, which constrains the Fed's path to
cutting** — the rate-path delay is the thesis-binding effect.

**Watch:** Trade representative announcements, USTR rule-makings,
specific tariff categories (lumber, steel, cement, copper).

**Direction:** − (negative for thesis through Factor 1 cost-side
pass-through and rate-path delay).

---

### Headwind 2 — Oil-driven inflation pass-through
**Status:** WTI elevated post-Iran war; Dallas Fed model implies
sustained $80+ through 2026 in base case.

**Mechanism.** Oil at $80+ implies energy CPI pass-through of +0.2-
0.3pp on core inflation, constraining Fed cut path. **This is the
single most-binding constraint on Factor 1 currently.** Oil pass-
through is the reason the FOMC's 2026 cut probability collapsed to
~3% [housing_context.json + Perplexity weekly 2026-05-04].

**Watch:** Strait of Hormuz situation, OPEC+ supply decisions,
inventory builds vs draws.

**Direction:** − (negative for thesis if sustained; positive if oil
falls below $70 sustained).

---

### Headwind 3 — Warsh anti-forward-guidance posture
**Status:** Fed Chair-confirmed; takes office June 2026.

**Mechanism.** Warsh's stated intent to abandon forward guidance and
the dot plot would mechanically widen MBS-Treasury spreads (forward
guidance was a transmission channel through which expected rate
paths got priced into MBS). **This is a Factor 1 negative
*independent of the rate path itself***.

**Watch:** Warsh confirmation hearing testimony, first FOMC press
conference in June, FOMC minutes language for absence of dot-plot
guidance.

**Direction:** − (mechanical; even if Fed cuts, mortgage rates may
not follow as cleanly).

## Election cycle layer

The 2026 mid-terms (November 2026) and 2028 presidential cycle
provide windows for legislative action on housing affordability. The
political-economy timeline:

```
2026:
  Q1-Q2:  Pre-mid-term legislative push (Section 121 expansion,
          first-time-buyer credit are most likely vehicles)
  Q3:     Election-season housing legislation framing intensifies
  Q4:     Post-election, lame-duck session — possible tax
          extenders package including Section 121 changes

2027:
  H1:     New Congress; housing legislation negotiation
  H2:     Implementation of any 2026-passed measures begins

2028:
  Q1-Q3:  Pre-presidential housing-affordability platform building
  Q4:     Election; transition uncertainty
```

**The thesis horizon (18-36 months from 2026-05-01) encompasses both
the 2026 mid-terms and the early-2028 presidential cycle.** Two
distinct windows for legislative tailwinds. The Perplexity Computer
weekly task's legislative-scuttlebutt section is positioned to
catch movement in either window.

## Counter-thesis on political economy

The strongest version of the bear case on Step 6:

**(1) Both major parties prefer status-quo housing policy.** The
political coalition for active legislative housing reform is
narrower than thesis-positive readings assume. Section 121 expansion
has been "imminent" for 5+ years without action. Assumable mortgage
reform has been "discussable" without director-level commitment.

**(2) Macro headwinds dominate micro tailwinds.** Tariffs + oil +
Warsh forward-guidance dynamics may all bind through 2027. The
quarter-by-quarter macro gravity overwhelms whatever marginal
legislative tailwinds materialize.

**(3) Legislative tailwinds may arrive but be sub-scale.** A
narrowed Section 121 expansion (e.g., $300K/$600K instead of full
inflation indexing) provides modest relief but doesn't materially
shift the lock-in math for the cohort that drives volume normalization.

**Counter to counter-thesis.** (1) is real but symmetric — the Section
121 freeze has been "imminent" without action because no
administration has needed it; the housing-affordability political
salience in 2026 is materially higher than in 2018-2024. (2) is
structurally true at the macro level but the micro tailwinds compound
with rate-path inflection when it arrives. (3) is the most-likely
scenario; sub-scale tailwinds still produce Factor-1 acceleration
relative to no-tailwind base case.

## What this section concludes

**Political economy is net-neutral on probability-weighted impact
basis but adds material *upside convexity* on the high-impact
positive levers (Section 121, assumable mortgages).** The thesis does
not require policy tailwinds to deliver returns. But policy tailwinds
arriving would meaningfully accelerate the timeline and increase
position sizing capacity.

The legislative-scuttlebutt monitor (Perplexity weekly + Script 14
news + Script 15 bill tracker) is the early-warning system that
distinguishes scenario A (tailwinds materialize → 12-month thesis
timeline) from scenario B (tailwinds don't → 24-36-month thesis
timeline). The position-build framework adapts to whichever scenario
the legislative tape confirms.
