# Step 2 — Demand Cohorts

> _Status: 2026-05-01. Default-driven first draft. ASSUMPTION-flagged
> on cohort-mobility numbers and bidirectional-Factor-4 framing._

The aggregate "housing demand" decomposition is the load-bearing read
in this section. Treating "the buyer" as a homogeneous entity loses the
Factor-4 ceiling effect entirely — the cohort whose homeownership is
declining is not the same cohort whose move-decisions drive the next
five years of transaction volume. The discipline is to decompose along
the dimension that matters [per Investment Research Training Manual
v5.0, L1088-1096]: age cohort, with mobility differential and
homeownership trajectory tracked separately per cohort.

The four cohorts that matter:

---

## Boomer (born 1946-1964; ages 62-80 in 2026)

**Population:** ~70M.
**Homeownership rate:** ~80% [Census Housing Vacancies and Homeownership].
**Mobility (turnover):** ~3-4%/yr — life-event driven (death, downsize,
assisted living).

**Position in the thesis.** This cohort is **structurally a seller of
existing-home inventory** over the next 10-20 years as it ages into the
75+ life stage. JCHS senior-housing analysis [Investment Research
Training Manual v5.0, L2059] documents the demographic wave:
"baby-boom cohort (1945-1955) now entering 80+ age range. During
COVID, demand crashed (infection fear, adult-child caregiving), and
supply development froze. Demand will inevitably exceed static
supply." The 80+ life-event triggers (assisted living, death of
spouse, single-person household formation) drive ~3-4%/yr of the
boomer-held housing stock to transact each year — and that transaction
rate accelerates as the cohort ages further.

**The Factor 1 lock-in is partly endogenous to this cohort.**
Boomer-held mortgages are disproportionately sub-4% (boomers refinanced
during 2020-2021 at scale; the Federal Reserve estimated ~50% of US
mortgage stock was at sub-4% rates by mid-2022). When a boomer ages
into the assisted-living trigger, the mortgage rate matters less than
the life event. The forced sale produces released inventory regardless
of where the 30yr is. **This creates a baseline "supply emergence"
floor that operates independent of Factor 1 unlock.**

**Implications:**
- The released inventory from boomer life-event transactions hits a
  market currently constrained by the affordability ceiling. The
  buyers for boomer homes are predominantly Gen X and older
  millennials (age 35-55) — the cohort that *can* afford the move
  but is rate-locked itself. Mismatch between supply emergence and
  demand re-extension is a key thesis tension.
- Senior housing operators (VTR, WELL) capture the move-out side of
  this cohort: each boomer move-out is a lease-up at a senior housing
  facility. **Senior housing is the inverse-correlated long against
  Factor 1.** Operates whether rates fall or stay high.
- The HCOL coastal markets (where boomers concentrated) face the
  largest absolute supply emergence. Apartment REITs' Tier-4 short
  basket (EQR, AVB) captures the resulting downward pressure on
  coastal rents as boomer-released SFR inventory competes with
  multifamily for the marginal renter.

## Gen X (born 1965-1980; ages 46-61 in 2026)

**Population:** ~65M.
**Homeownership rate:** ~75% [Census].
**Mobility (turnover):** ~3-4%/yr — peak earning years; primary moves
are right-sizing (kids out of house, downsize).

**Position in the thesis.** **Gen X is the rate-locked-anchored
cohort.** They bought their primary residences during 2010-2020, mostly
at sub-5% rates, often at sub-4%. Right-sizing moves are deferrable —
no immediate life-event forcing. Gen X is the cohort whose mobility
math is most directly governed by Factor 1: when rates fall enough
that the spread closes, they move; until then, they stay.

**This is the single largest cohort whose transaction rate is rate-
endogenous.** ~65M people × 75% homeownership × ~3.5%/yr mobility =
~1.7M Gen-X-driven transactions in a normalized year. At current
rates, perhaps half that pace materializes — the rate-locked Gen X
who would right-size is sitting still. **The unlocked Gen X cohort is
the largest single demand-emergence segment when Factor 1 fires.**

The reciprocal effect: when Gen X moves, they're moving up in the size
distribution (kids-out-of-house downsize is the exception; the median
move is *upgrade* not downsize). They *create* additional demand for
the larger homes the boomer cohort is releasing. **Gen X is the
demographic match for boomer supply.**

## Millennial (born 1981-1996; ages 30-45 in 2026)

**Population:** ~72M.
**Homeownership rate:** **37.1% (under-35) / 61.8% (35-44)** —
declining [JCHS State of the Nation's Housing 2025, p. 25].
**Mobility (turnover):** ~5-6%/yr — peak first-time-buyer cohort, peak
career and family transitions.

**Position in the thesis.** **Millennials are the cohort the
affordability ceiling binds on.** JCHS 2025 documents the breakdown:
median first-time-buyer age has hit a record-high 38, requiring
$126,700 income on a $412,500 median home, with $26,800 cash for
down-payment + closing — only 12% of renters can meet the cash
requirement [JCHS p. 27].

The "missing households" dynamic [JCHS p. 18] is millennial-specific:
the 2008-2014 Great Recession delayed millennial household formation
by ~250k/yr. The 2019-2023 catch-up phase (post-COVID) saw those
delayed formations materialize, lifting headship rates temporarily.
**That tailwind ended in 2024.** The rate of millennial household
formation is now driven entirely by underlying population (cohort
aging) — and the affordability gate determines what fraction of those
formations is owner vs renter.

**Implications:**
- Millennials are the demand cohort that *should* absorb the supply
  released by boomer/Gen-X move-out. But the affordability ceiling
  has narrowed the qualifying portion of the cohort to perhaps half
  of historical levels. **Even if Factor 1 fires fully, the
  absorbing capacity is structurally smaller than thesis-classical
  models assume.**
- The under-35 homeownership rate at 37.1% (vs ~40% long-run average)
  represents both a deficit (potential mean-reversion = ~2.9pp
  improvement = ~2-3M households shifting to ownership at full
  cohort capacity) AND a structural ceiling (if 37.1% is the new
  reality, mean reversion doesn't happen).
- The bidirectional Factor-4 framing: bull case = mean reversion;
  bear case = structural shift. The framework prose treats Factor 4
  as bidirectional pending more data; Step 5 stock-scoring
  appropriately discounts confidence on the absorption side of the
  thesis.

## Gen Z (born 1997-2012; ages 14-29 in 2026)

**Population:** ~68M.
**Homeownership rate:** Material data not yet meaningful at the cohort
level (most are pre-formation).
**Mobility (turnover):** N/A — predominantly renting; first-time
formation just beginning.

**Position in the thesis.** **The cohort whose formation rate
determines the next 10-year demand baseline.** JCHS 2025's main
projection of 8.6M new households 2025-2035 [p. 20] embeds an
assumption about Gen Z headship rates. If Gen Z forms households at the
millennial pace (lower than historical), the JCHS number stands. If
Gen Z formation is *materially worse* than millennial, the JCHS
projection is itself optimistic.

**Two indicators worth tracking:**

1. **Gen Z headship rate at age 25.** This is the leading signal of
   structural shift. Comparable cohorts: 1994 (Gen X at 25) headship
   ~38%; 2010 (millennial at 25) headship ~30%; 2026 (Gen Z at 25)
   headship — currently uncertain, JCHS hasn't published this
   sub-cohort cut yet.
2. **Gen Z student debt service ratio.** If student loan repayment
   resumes at scale (forbearance ended 2024) and the ratio of debt
   service to income compresses Gen Z's downpayment savings rate,
   the affordability ceiling for the *next* first-time-buyer cohort
   gets worse, not better — extending Factor 4's bear read by 5-10
   years.

**Implications for thesis horizon:** Gen Z primarily matters for the
*sustainability* of the thesis post-unwind. The unlock catalyst (Factor
1 falling rates) drives 2026-2028 transaction volume. The demand
*level* in 2030-2035 depends on Gen Z formation rates, which depend
on affordability, student debt, and household-economics fundamentals
that current policy doesn't address. **The thesis is right for the
next 3 years but possibly wrong for the next 10.**

## Cohort cross-tabulation

The transaction matrix — who sells to whom — drives the thesis flow.

| Seller cohort | Likely buyer cohort | Frequency | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boomer (age-out) | Gen X (right-size up) or older millennial (FTHB) | Highest now | Boomer death/AL → released SFR → bid by Gen X or upgrading millennial |
| Gen X (right-size) | Older millennial (FTHB) | Increasing 2026-2028 | Rate-locked Gen X waiting for unlock; when fires, releases first-time-buyer-grade homes |
| Millennial (move-up) | Younger millennial / Gen Z (FTHB) | Increasing 2027-2030 | Compounds Factor 1 unlock 2-3 years lagged |
| Gen Z (FTHB) | N/A (entering market) | Marginal | Demand baseline for 2030+ |

**The thesis-positive transaction is Boomer→Gen X or Gen X→Millennial.**
Both depend on the buyer-cohort qualifying (Factor 5 affordability) and
the seller-cohort transacting (Factor 1 lock-in unwind for Gen X;
demographic-wave for Boomer). The two channels accelerate together at
the rate-path inflection.

The thesis-negative transaction is Boomer→Millennial — released supply
that the affordability-constrained millennial cohort cannot absorb,
producing price decline rather than volume normalization. **This is the
counter-thesis on Factor 1 (Step 5 scoring weights this risk).**

## Mobility differential summary

For Step 5 stock-scoring, the cohort weights matter:

```
weighted_demand_emergence = 
    boomer_population × homeownership × mobility_rate × age_acceleration
  + gen_x_population × homeownership × mobility_rate × rate_path_factor  
  + millennial_population × homeownership × mobility_rate × affordability_factor
  + gen_z_population × headship_rate × first_time_buyer_factor
```

_ASSUMPTION:_ The relative weights for the four cohorts in the
unlock-volume forecast are proxied by population × homeownership ×
turnover. Gen X is the largest single contributor at ~1.7M annualized
transactions in normalized scenario; boomer is ~1.5M; millennial is
~3M (with the ceiling-discount applied); Gen Z is <0.5M emerging.
**Total ~6.7M scenario** vs 4.0M current pace — implies ~70% normalization
upside, conservative on the cohort side and aggressive on the unlock
mechanism side.

The Step 5 scoring uses these weights to discount thesis-exposure of
names that disproportionately serve the affordability-constrained
millennial cohort (entry-level builders LGIH/CCS) — they're long-thesis
names but with elevated Factor-4 risk that the demand absorption
doesn't materialize at the volume the supply releases.
