## Key risk

The dominant risk this week is the **structural change in how rate expectations get priced**, not the rate level itself. With Powell's last meeting on April 29 and Warsh succeeding in June, two simultaneous forces are now squeezing Factor 1: (a) the realized cut path collapsed (CME 2026-cut probability now ~3%), and (b) Warsh's stated intent to abandon forward guidance and the dot plot will likely *widen* the MBS-Treasury spread by removing one of the channels through which expected paths compress that spread. Mortgage spreads have already been 200-220 bps in recent weeks against a 160-180 bps historical average — and the bond market is signaling persistent inflation (10yr Treasury 4.39%, 30yr 4.96% per March 20 reference). If oil sustains >$80 through Q2-Q3 (Dallas Fed base case), every ingredient for the 30yr to retrace toward 5.5% is moving the wrong direction.

The thesis-falsifying observation: if this week's data is the *new normal* — operating names selling off on rate-down moves because the discount-rate / forward-path channel dominates the spot-rate channel — then the thesis is right about the lockup but wrong about the unlock catalyst. The locked-low owners stay locked, but the long-side beneficiaries don't re-rate even when rates eventually fall, because by then the affordability-ceiling cohort (Factor 5, now Factor 4 ceiling) has been hollowed out for so long that the absorbed pent-up demand is smaller than the thesis assumes.

The single number to watch: **CME FedWatch 2026 cut probability**. If it stays below 10% through May, downgrade thesis time horizon by 6 months in next quarterly review. If oil sustains >$80 through July, downgrade further.

## Underappreciated catalyst

The **FHFA/HUD VantageScore 4.0 + FICO 10T approval (April 22)** is being framed by mainstream coverage as a credit-scoring story — incremental, technical, slow-burn. The second-order effect that consensus is missing is on the **Factor 4 (demographics ceiling) thesis specifically**.

Three pieces of evidence consensus is not connecting:
1. **VantageScore 4.0 explicitly incorporates rent-payment history,** which expands the qualifiable-borrower pool by an estimated 4-7M consumers (per the long-running policy-intent record cited by Brownstein).
2. **Pulte's "stay tuned" on LLPAs.** When asked about Loan-Level Price Adjustments, Director Pulte said pricing grids will be released separately for VantageScore 4.0 loans. Translation: the GSEs are reserving the option to *price* the new-cohort loans differently from FICO loans, which means lenders' GSE delivery economics change for this cohort.
3. **Historical FICO 10T data publishes summer 2026** for loans acquired Apr 2013 → Sep 2025. If loss-rate distributions show parity or better with Classic FICO at equivalent score, the policy moves from theoretical to live by Q4. Lenders adopt the new score not because regulators told them to, but because the LLPA pricing makes it accretive.

Consensus position (per JCHS 2025): household formation 8.6M for 2025-2035, a ~30% step-down from prior decadal rates. This forecast does **not** appear to incorporate the credit-eligibility expansion. If even 30% of the newly-qualifiable cohort converts to first-time buyers over 3-5 years, JCHS's number is too low by ~1-2M households.

The contrarian read: while Factor 1 (rate path) is weakening for the next 6-12 months on the FOMC + Warsh transition, Factor 4 (demographics ceiling) is structurally improving on the credit-policy side — and that's a multi-year tailwind, not a quarter tailwind. Long-side names with high Factor 4 sensitivity (entry-level builders LGIH/CCS once the cancel pulse stabilizes; FHA-cohort originators; manufactured housing/MH lots) are *cheaper now* than they would be if consensus correctly priced the credit-policy tailwind.

Specific watch: **summer 2026 FICO 10T historical-data publication** is the catalyst. If GSE pricing grids (LLPAs) for VantageScore 4.0 print at parity or *below* equivalent Classic FICO, Tier-1 originators with FHA exposure (LDI if it survives, RKT, UWMC) re-rate within a quarter on the share-shift mechanics alone.
