## Bottom line / binding constraint this week

The binding constraint this week is **the policy-path widening of MBS spreads, not the spot 30yr.** Spot rates eased; the path to 5.5% extended. Powell's last meeting was April 29; Warsh succeeds in June. Two FOMC channels that historically compressed term premia — forward guidance, the dot plot — are scheduled for removal. This is the structural backdrop against which everything else (LGIH cohort fracture, builder cohort closing-throughput weakness, apartment REIT outperformance) makes sense as a single picture rather than disparate signals: **rates higher for longer, the supply-unlock catalyst pushed out, the demand ceiling biting first.**

The thesis is not broken. The unlock math (Factor 1) is intact: any path to 5.5% still releases 12.6M homes / 3.5M SAAR uplift; any path to 5.0% releases 21.3M / 6.0M SAAR. What changed is the **slope of the path,** not the destination. The position implication for an analyst (not a recommendation): the long-thesis names need to clear a longer runway with thinner balance sheets, and the cohort that fractures first (entry-level / FHA-leveraged / LGIH-style) is fracturing now.

**Reading the curve.** The unlock is **step-functional, not smooth**. Each FHFA distribution bucket whose midpoint crosses (market_rate − 1.5%) releases at once. From today, the next material unlock requires a **drop to 5.75%** (releases the 4.5-5.0% bucket — 7.2M homes, 2.0M SAAR). The deepest-locked cohort (sub-3% mortgages, 11.1M homes) requires a 30yr below 4.5% — well outside the thesis horizon at any non-recession path.

**The arithmetic from current spot (6.30%) to the first unlock trigger (5.50%) is 80 bps.** Two paths:

1. **Fed cut path.** Each 25-bp Fed cut maps to ~15 bps of 30yr move (historical sensitivity, [MBA chart-of-the-week]). 80 bps requires ~5 cuts — but 2026 cut probability is now ~3% per [CME FedWatch](https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/interest-rates/cme-fedwatch-tool.html), and Warsh's removal of forward guidance likely raises term premia not lowers them. Path is closed for 2026.
2. **MBS-spread compression path.** Mortgage spreads at 200-220 bps vs 160-180 bps historical. 50 bps of spread compression (no Fed move) would deliver the 30yr drop. This requires (a) MBS supply absorption — depends on Fed's taper trajectory and any return to net MBS purchases (the Trump $200B MBS directive [Politico Jan 8](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/01/08/trump-mortgage-fannie-freddie-00717985) is in this category), (b) bank/REIT MBS demand — depends on bank capital relief (EO 14393 [White House Mar 13](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/03/) directs revising mortgage risk weights), and (c) reduced uncertainty premium — works *against* Warsh's posture. Net: not closed, but slow.

**Confidence: high on the math, medium on the path.** The mechanics are deterministic; the path arithmetic is policy-conditional.

## Q3 — Inventory paradox

**The paradox.** Existing-home inventory is rising (964,477 active listings, +49,617 vs 4w; [Realtor.com / housing_context](https://www.realtor.com/research/data/)). New-home permits are falling (1,372k SAAR, −166k vs 4w). NAR pending sales are negative YoY. So which is binding — supply unlock or demand collapse?

**Decomposition.**

| Channel | Direction | Magnitude | Read |
|---|:--:|---|---|
| **Existing-home active listings** | rising | +5% in 4w | Recently-locked cohort (5-6.5%) crossing the threshold incrementally as time-anchored move-frictions accumulate (job change, family event, aging into downsize); also some delisted re-listing |
| **Months of supply** (existing) | rising slightly | ~3.0 mo (Q1) | Historic norm 4-5 mo; still tight |
| **Median DOM** | rising | est. 50-55 days vs 40 last spring | Listings lingering — not absorbing |
| **Price-cut share** | rising | est. 18-20% (Realtor.com) | Marginal pricing power weakening |
| **List-to-sale price ratio** | falling | est. 98-99% vs 99.5%+ trend | Buyers gaining negotiating leverage on the margin |
| **New-listings flow** | rising | NAR commentary | More owners testing the market |
| **New-home permits** | falling | −166k SAAR vs 4w | Builders pulling back on forward issuance |
| **Builder cancel rates** | rising at entry-level only (LGIH 45.6, Redfin 13.4) | divergent | FHA cohort breaking, move-up cohort firm |

**Synthesis.** This is **NOT** a "unlock catalyst is firing" picture (no Factor 1 trigger crossed; locked count unchanged). It IS a "**marginal supply rising into weakening marginal demand**" picture — the worst of both for short-term price dynamics. The existing-home seller is increasingly willing to list (driven by life events, not rates); the marginal entry-level buyer is increasingly unable to qualify. Two soft trend lines crossing in opposite directions.

**Implication for the thesis.** The thesis depends on rates falling enough to **simultaneously** unlock supply *and* relieve P&I-driven affordability pressure on demand. If supply rises before demand recovers (this week's regime), prices fall — a healthy thesis-positive outcome on a 24-36-month horizon (it heals affordability faster) but **thesis-negative on a 6-12 month horizon** (it implies a 5-10% nominal price correction first, builder cohort takes operational hits, FHA delinquency continues to rise, and Tier-1 originator + builder names face Q2-Q3 earnings pressure).

**Confidence: high on the trajectory, low on the timing.** The micro-data (price cuts, DOM, list-to-sale) supports the read; the timing of when unlock catalyst meets the demand recovery is the hardest part of the thesis to forecast.

## Underappreciated catalyst (multi-paragraph + time-to-tape tag)

### **VantageScore 4.0 + FICO 10T historical loss-rate distributions, due summer 2026**

**Time-to-tape: 60-120 days (loss-rate publication summer 2026; lender adoption signaling 2026 H2-2027 H1).**

The April 22 FHFA + HUD authorization of VantageScore 4.0 and FICO 10T is being framed by mainstream coverage as a credit-scoring competition story. Three pieces of evidence consensus is *not* connecting that make this a Factor 4 catalyst:

**1. VantageScore 4.0 incorporates rent-payment history into scoring.** Per the [FHFA April 22 release](https://www.fhfa.gov/news/news-release/homebuying-advances-into-new-era-of-credit-score-competition) and [Brownstein analysis](https://www.bhfs.com/insight/fhfa-and-hud-greenlight-credit-scoring-changes/), VantageScore 4.0 has been engineered to incorporate non-credit data including rent history. Industry estimates (per VantageScore's own white papers and McKinsey analyses) suggest this expands the qualifiable-borrower pool by **4-7M consumers** — primarily renters who've been paying on time but have no traditional credit footprint to quantify.

**2. Pulte's "stay tuned" on LLPAs.** FHFA Director William Pulte signaled at the April 22 release event that GSE Loan-Level Price Adjustments (LLPAs) for new-cohort loans (those scored on VantageScore 4.0 or FICO 10T) may be priced differently — accretively — for lenders who adopt early. **The implication:** GSEs are reserving the option to price the new-cohort loans more favorably, creating a pricing incentive for early lender adoption.

**3. Historical FICO 10T data publishes summer 2026.** FICO 10T has historical loss-rate distributions that haven't been published yet but are due summer 2026 per the FHFA timeline. **If the loss-rate distributions show parity with FICO Classic (i.e., FICO 10T loans don't default at higher rates),** lenders adopt because LLPA pricing is accretive.

**Why consensus is missing this:** Mainstream credit-scoring coverage focuses on the headline competition between VantageScore and FICO; the read-through to *housing demand* is treated as ancillary. But the second-order effect — 4-7M new qualified borrowers in 2026 H2 / 2027 H1 — directly attacks the demographics ceiling (Factor 4) at exactly the moment when entry-level cohort qualification is binding hardest. The cohort that's currently driving LGIH's 45.6% cancel rate is precisely the cohort that VantageScore 4.0 + rent-payment-history scoring is designed to absorb.
